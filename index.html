I prepared for the CSS exam by delving deeply into the nitty gritty of each subject, approaching every topic from multiple perspectives. In the compulsory subjects, my Islamiat paper, which I considered one of my best, was failed with 39 marks. In Precis, I narrowly missed the passing score, failing by a margin of just 4 marks. Most disappointing was my Essay paper, where I scored only 30 marks despite putting in serious effort. In contrast, several of my friends, who relied on a handful of quotations, statistics, and paper attempting techniques, scored far higher. Their knowledge of the subjects was superficial at best. Meanwhile, I had studied a wide range of books, research articles, and documentaries to gain depth and originality in my answers. This experience leaves only one conclusion. Performance in CSS does not reflect intellectual depth or academic strength. Examiners prefer a certain presentation style that includes diagrams, flowcharts, quotations, and statistics. As a result, candidates with surface level preparation often outscore those who pursue deeper and critical study. I am left with mixed feelings. It is disheartening to realize that hard work, depth of knowledge, and analytical reasoning are not recognized. At the same time, it is evident that CSS rewards conformity to examiner expectations rather than merit. The process has become a lottery, not an objective evaluation of one’s abilities.
